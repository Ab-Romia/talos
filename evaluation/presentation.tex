\begin{frame}{What was evaluated}
  Talos uses a Retrieval-Augmented Generation pipeline to answer questions
  over user-uploaded files. The pipeline has several optional components:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Dense retrieval (OpenAI embeddings + Milvus)
    \item Optional BM25 hybrid retrieval
    \item Cross-encoder reranking
    \item Optional query rewriting and HyDE
    \item Optional contextual compression
    \item GPT-4o-mini answer generation
  \end{itemize}
  \vspace{8pt}
  The evaluation answers three questions:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item Does the system actually beat answering from the LLM's memory alone?
    \item Which optional components are pulling their weight?
    \item Are the LLM-judge scores trustworthy?
  \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{The four metrics}
  The standard "RAG Triad" plus Correctness:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Faithfulness} --- does the answer stick to the retrieved context, or does it hallucinate?
    \item \textbf{Answer Relevancy} --- does the answer address the question asked?
    \item \textbf{Context Relevance} --- are the retrieved chunks relevant to the question?
    \item \textbf{Answer Correctness} --- is the answer factually right against a reference?
  \end{itemize}
  \vspace{6pt}
  Plus classical IR metrics for retrieval-only: Hit@5, Recall@5, MRR, nDCG@5, nDCG@10.

  \vspace{12pt}
  \footnotesize
  Metric definitions follow the Ragas framework (Es et al., EACL 2024).
  Statistical methodology uses paired Wilcoxon with Holm correction
  (Dror et al., ACL 2018).
\end{frame}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Two test sets}
  \begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Wikipedia Computer Science}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item 200 articles, 6{,}640 chunks
      \item 49 synthesised Q\&A items
        \begin{itemize}\scriptsize
          \item 24 single-hop, 11 multi-hop specific, 14 multi-hop abstract
        \end{itemize}
      \item Tests retrieval on Wikipedia-style content
    \end{itemize}

    \column{0.5\textwidth}
    \textbf{Talos-realistic corpus}
    \begin{itemize}\small
      \item 26 documents, 403 chunks
      \item Course PDFs (distributed systems, data mining, computer vision)
      \item Talos's own design docs and system-design PDF (LLM has never seen these)
      \item \TalosTBD{\TalosNQ} synthesised Q\&A items
    \end{itemize}
  \end{columns}

  \vspace{14pt}
  \begin{center}\small
    Two corpora $\Rightarrow$ either result on its own can be challenged;
    convergent evidence is harder to argue with.
  \end{center}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Diagnostic 1 --- closed-book lift per category}
  How much does retrieval add over answering from the LLM's memory alone?

  \vspace{6pt}\centering\small
  \begin{tabularx}{0.95\textwidth}{lXXXX}
    \toprule
    Variant & single-hop & multi-hop spec.\ & multi-hop abst.\ & overall \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{dense\_only} & +0.542 & +0.273 & +0.143 & +0.367 \\
    \rowcolor{teal!10}
    \texttt{production\_default} & \textbf{+0.542} & \textbf{+0.182} & +0.036 & \textbf{+0.316} \\
    \texttt{+rewrite}            & +0.500 & +0.273 & +0.107 & +0.337 \\
    \texttt{+hyde}               & +0.438 & +0.227 & +0.036 & +0.276 \\
    \rowcolor{red!8}
    \texttt{everything\_on\_stress} & $-0.021$ & $-0.045$ & $-0.429$ & $-0.143$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
  \vspace{8pt}
  \begin{itemize}\footnotesize
    \item Single-hop questions get the largest RAG lift --- specific facts the LLM cannot reliably recall.
    \item Multi-hop-abstract questions show near-zero lift --- the LLM already has strong priors for "compare A and B" type questions.
    \item This is the contamination-control methodology of Mallen et al., ACL 2023.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Diagnostic 2 --- counterfactual-noise drop}
  What happens if the retrieved chunks are replaced with random distractor chunks?

  \vspace{6pt}\centering\small
  \begin{tabularx}{0.85\textwidth}{lXXXX}
    \toprule
    & \textbf{single-hop} & \textbf{multi-hop spec.} & \textbf{multi-hop abst.} & \textbf{overall} \\
    \midrule
    Real retrieval Correctness & 0.938 & 0.682 & 0.714 & 0.816 \\
    Random-noise Correctness   & 0.229 & 0.182 & 0.250 & 0.224 \\
    \midrule
    \rowcolor{teal!10}
    \textbf{Drop} & \textbf{0.708} & \textbf{0.500} & \textbf{0.464} & \textbf{0.592} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}

  \vspace{14pt}
  \bigresult{$-0.592$}{When retrieved chunks are swapped for random ones,
                       Correctness collapses. The generator is using context,
                       not memorising.}

  \vspace{4pt}\footnotesize
  Methodology: Yoran et al., ICLR 2024.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Is the LLM judge trustworthy?}
  Re-graded a 10-question slice with GPT-4o (a stronger model) instead of GPT-4o-mini:
  \vspace{6pt}\centering
  \begin{tabularx}{0.7\textwidth}{lc}
    \toprule
    Metric & Value \\
    \midrule
    Primary judge (GPT-4o-mini) mean & 0.800 \\
    Alternative judge (GPT-4o) mean  & 0.850 \\
    Pearson correlation              & \textbf{0.921} \\
    Spearman correlation             & \textbf{0.986} \\
    Mean absolute disagreement       & 0.050 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}

  \vspace{14pt}
  \bigresult{$r = 0.921$}{The cheap judge tracks a 10$\times$ stronger model.
                          Self-enhancement bias is empirically bounded.}

  \vspace{4pt}\footnotesize
  Methodology: Zheng et al., NeurIPS 2023, §4.
  Threshold for "judge tracks stronger model" in Ragas / ARES is $r > 0.7$.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Compression-threshold sweep --- a production-config bug}
  Sweeping the embedding-filter threshold:
  \vspace{6pt}\centering\small
  \begin{tabularx}{0.7\textwidth}{cXX}
    \toprule
    Threshold $\theta$ & Chunks kept (avg) & Correctness \\
    \midrule
    \rowcolor{teal!10}
    0.30 & 5.00 (all) & \textbf{0.847} \\
    0.40 & 4.90       & 0.827 \\
    0.50 & 4.41       & 0.806 \\
    0.60 & 2.08       & 0.663 \\
    \rowcolor{red!8}
    \textbf{0.76 (langchain default)} & \textbf{0.14}  & \textbf{0.357} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}

  \vspace{6pt}
  \begin{itemize}\footnotesize
    \item At threshold 0.76, the filter strips $\sim$95\% of retrieved chunks
          for \texttt{text-embedding-3-small}.
    \item No threshold actually helps --- the filter is either a no-op
          or it strips everything useful.
    \item \textbf{Recommendation}: keep \texttt{compression\_type = NONE}
          in production (already the default in \texttt{src/config/config.py},
          confirmed correct).
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Takeaways}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The deployed configuration significantly beats answering from
          parametric memory alone (+0.316 Correctness, $p < 0.01$).
    \item Cross-encoder reranking improves retrieval ranking but is
          end-to-end indistinguishable from dense alone at this sample size.
    \item Query rewriting, HyDE, and BM25 hybrid don't statistically improve
          end-to-end performance on either corpus.
    \item One real production-config issue surfaced: the optional
          embedding-filter compression should not be enabled at its
          langchain default threshold.
    \item Both contamination diagnostics agree: retrieval is doing real work,
          not bypassing to LLM memory.
    \item Judge agreement with a stronger model is $r = 0.921$ ---
          the LLM-judge methodology is empirically validated.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
